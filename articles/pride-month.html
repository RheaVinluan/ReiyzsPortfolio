The Only Proud That Matters

Published on: The QCU Times Publication

3... 2... 1... Roll.

The director’s voice pierced the air like a starting gun, and suddenly the spotlight was mine alone. I sat on a velvet blue couch beneath the warmth of stage lights that didn’t quite reach the cold corners of my heart.

I smiled — a curve drawn carefully across my lips, painted in a red so fierce it almost hid the tremble beneath.

"Happy Pride", I began.

The words floated out of me, bright and polished, as if I hadn’t rehearsed them a hundred times in the mirror — as if my voice hadn’t once trembled in a bedroom lit only by shame.

My eyes gleamed, not from joy, but from a memory — of another gaze I once sought: His.

Applause filled the studio, but it echoed like silence. Because the only clap I ever longed for—the one that truly mattered— never came.

“June has come again,” I said, my voice louder now. “Pride Month. A celebration. But also a resistance. A march. A prayer. A promise. We’ve come so far. We still have so far to go."

The camera caught every word. Every emotion. Every proud confession dressed in rainbow hope.

When the shoot ended, we clapped. We smiled. We shouted "Happy Pride!" as if it had always been that easy.

But when the lights went down and the crowd scattered, I packed my things in silence.

I went home.

Home.

A word too soft to describe four walls filled with memories of absence.

Is it home, if no one is waiting?

He left. Left me stranded in my own truth. Left me unseen, unheard, unloved.

He left behind only one photo. His smile frozen in time, while mine has had to keep reshaping itself — into strength, into survival.

The curtains still sway with his presence.
His laughter still dances faintly through the corners.

But so do the echoes.
His shouts.
His disappointment.
His cold silence.

And beneath them all, the question that bloomed in the darkest soil of my soul: Is being gay a sin?

The world said no.
The parades, the hashtags, the colors in the sky — they all said no.
But he never said anything.

Until one day — after the vlog aired.

It exploded.
I exploded.
They said I was brave.
They said I was radiant.
They called me trailblazer, icon, light.
But not him.

The morning after, as I toasted my bread in a kitchen that had grown too quiet for joy, the doorbell rang. I opened it to a ghost. He stood there — weathered, unsure, voice softer than I remembered.

“Do you mind if I come in?” he asked.
Oh, God. That voice. I nodded.

He entered the room like he was stepping into a memory he wasn’t sure still belonged to him. He sat. We stared. Time suspended between our glances. And then— he sighed, the kind of breath that sounds like surrender and love all at once.

"I'm proud of you... my child."
