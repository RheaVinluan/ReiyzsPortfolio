So this is love…

Published on: The QCU Times Publication

There were arched smiles that lit the corners of rooms like sun through stained glass—fleeting, but vivid. Laughter, soft and spontaneous, would rise like warm wind after rain. We built moments like secret fortresses—chaotic, unplanned, yet sacred. The days passed like pages turned with care, where hearts found shelter, and heavy moods learned to float again.

Even in the midst of disarray—in the hush beneath stormy skies, lying side by side in the grass—I saw beauty. In him. In us. In something unspoken.

As a child, she used to wonder what love truly meant. Beyond dictionary definitions, beyond poetic lines etched in borrowed books or whispered across glowing screens—what did it feel like?

Does it come with pain?
Is it always joyful?
Does it mean being beside someone, always?

But time, as it always does, offered answers. Sometimes quietly. Sometimes all at once. The heart, once so sure that love had to be claimed, learned that it could also be released—and still remain.

Love, she found, is safety. Not the kind that locks you in, but the kind that softens your edges. It might ache—not out of cruelty, but because two souls, no matter how kind, are flawed. Yet love returns—not to fix, but to hold. To stay kind.

Love is not always light. Sometimes, it sits beside you in the dark, speaking only through the hush of a breath or the warmth of a silent presence. It arrives in laughter or lingers in tears—because the world, after all, is not always tender. But love is. Love is the gentleness in a pause. In a word. In a goodbye that still carries warmth.

And no, love isn’t always in the staying. Sometimes, it lives in the distance—in watching him become who he’s meant to be, even if that path doesn’t lead back to her. Because love does not cling; it blesses. It sets free. It says, even if you are not mine, I hope you find peace.

So this is love.
Not loud. Not claimed. But real.

Gentle.
Kind.
Steady.
Healing.

A love that lived quietly in the folds of the moments we shared—and still lives, even now, without needing a name.
