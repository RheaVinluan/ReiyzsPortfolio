Never Again, Never Forget: Why September 21st Must Be Remembered

Published on: The QCU Times Publication

September 21 is not just another date on the calendar—it is a wound in our nation’s history, one that continues to bleed each time we try to ignore it. On this day in 1972, Proclamation No. 1081 was signed, plunging the Philippines into martial law. Two days later, its official announcement unleashed an era of fear, repression, and unbridled greed. Should we ever dare to forget, we risk repeating the same nightmare.

Under late President Ferdinand Marcos Sr., democracy was not merely eroded—it was dismantled. The regime peddled the illusion of stability while systematically silencing dissent, crushing institutions, and looting the nation dry. The Guinness World Records did not mince words, branding the Marcos plunder the “greatest robbery of a government.” Billions of dollars—money that should have funded schools, hospitals, and jobs—vanished into the pockets of the powerful. How many generations have already paid for this theft?

The numbers alone are damning. Amnesty International documented over 11,000 victims of torture, killings, and disappearances. In his own words, Marcos Sr. admitted that more than 50,000 people were arrested—church leaders, lawyers, workers, journalists—people who dared to resist. Was “order” worth the price of blood? Was “progress” worth the screams silenced in the dead of night?

Martial law was not just a dictatorship; it was a betrayal. A betrayal of trust, of democracy, of the very idea that government exists to serve the people. Instead, those in power treated governance as a family business and the nation’s wealth as their personal vault. And today, do we not see the same familiar faces—political dynasties recycling themselves, elites flaunting wealth while millions remain hungry? Have we learned nothing?

It is infuriating that decades after martial law, corruption still thrives like a malignant tumor in our institutions. Public servants strut in excess while the poor line up for aid that barely sustains them. Do we not owe it to the victims of martial law to resist this cycle of impunity? Or shall we, in our silence, become accomplices to history’s repetition?

September 21 should burn into our collective memory—not just as a cautionary tale, but as a call to vigilance. Democracy is not self-sustaining; it demands that we speak out, resist injustice, and hold leaders accountable. To shrug it off as “the past” is to mock those who suffered and died.

“Never again” should not be a hollow slogan. It should be our rallying cry. Never again to tyranny. Never again to the silencing of the oppressed. Never again to the looting of our future. If we fail to protect the truth, then we are not just forgetting history—we are betraying it.

This September 21, the choice is ours: do we honor the memory of martial law’s victims by defending democracy, or do we bury them under the weight of our indifference?
