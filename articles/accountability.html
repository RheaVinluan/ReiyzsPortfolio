Accountability Exempts No One

Published on: The QCU Times Publication

Collaborative Article

In the wake of this year’s State of the Nation Address (SONA) held on July 28, 2025, it’s essential to reflect on a critical question: Why do accountability standards diverge when it comes to our leaders? The phrase “Wala namang perpektong presidente” or “There’s no perfect president” is often invoked to justify shortcomings in leadership. Yet, in other professions—medicine, law, engineering—individuals are rigorously held accountable. A doctor faces the consequences of a tragic loss on the operating table. A lawyer is scrutinized for legal failures that jeopardize lives. Engineers are liable when their projects fail. So why, then, should a president be perceived as exempt from scrutiny?

A SONA is not merely a speech—it is a covenant with the people. It is expected to articulate both achievements and the responsibilities that leaders owe to their constituents. When critical issues are overlooked, it reveals a governance style more preoccupied with appearance than accountability.

At first glance, President Marcos Jr.’s 4th SONA projected optimism. With mountains of taxpayers’ money poured into infrastructure and flood control projects, the government appeared to be prioritizing one of the country’s most persistent nightmares. In fact, the Department of Public Works and Highways (DPWH) received a ₱1.007 trillion budget for 2025—second only to the Department of Education. On paper, this reflects recognition that flooding is a long-term threat to lives, livelihoods, and the economy.

It should be good news, especially for those living in flood-prone areas. Yet, if funding is massive and continues, why do the same streets flood year after year? Despite the impressive budget figures, the reality on the ground tells a different story. Homes remain submerged, and lives are disrupted. In July 2025 alone, the Philippines experienced severe flooding due to consecutive typhoons. No amount of fancy figures can wash away the frustration of Filipinos trapped—literally and figuratively—in the floodwaters of government failure.

The Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration (PAGASA) has said that flooding stems from both natural and man-made factors. While meteorological and seismic events are unavoidable, man-made calamities are born of poor planning, broken promises, and bloated budgets that fail to deliver results. When the president labels disaster as the “new normal,” it sounds less like foresight and more like a eulogy for accountability. It suggests a government resigned to negligence, wrapped in fatalism.

It’s easy to point fingers—at corrupt contractors, complacent local officials, or inefficient agencies. In his statement, “...mahiya naman kayo sa inyong kapwa Pilipino!” he called out his corrupt officials. However, the sitting president should not just observe from the sidelines. Shouldn’t he also feel ashamed for failing to recognize this issue before it escalated into such significant harm for the Filipino people? He, too, is responsible for ensuring that these massive funds result in real, working solutions—not just audits and flashy ribbon-cutting ceremonies. Filipinos aren’t asking for miracles. They are asking for drainage that works, roads that don’t turn into rivers, and homes that don’t drown in deep promises

Marcos’ vow to release a list of flood control projects for public scrutiny may sound like a step toward transparency. But what people need is not just transparency—they need consequences. Acknowledging a problem publicly is only the beginning. The Filipino people deserve more than high-budget programs. We deserve a government that transforms billions into real protection—not just from rain, but from a system that keeps letting them drown.

Climate resilience saw new measures—evacuation centers, early warning systems, and flood project audits. The President’s call for transparency and naming of ghost projects is bold. But the real test is in follow-through. Will public shaming lead to accountability, or merely fade into another cycle of finger-pointing?

Meanwhile, Marcos vowed to veto any budget that diverges from the National Expenditure Program. It’s a signal of fiscal discipline that could limit pork-laden allocations—but without contingency plans, such moves risk halting essential services, especially during disasters.

On infrastructure, the president laid out grand visions—new roads, bridges, and transit projects. But many remain incomplete due to right-of-way disputes and weak management. Projects like the South Luzon Expressway Toll Road 4 (SLEX TR4) and the Davao–Samal bridge continue to stall amid lawsuits and environmental issues. A vision inspires, but credibility demands verified milestones—not just ribbon cuttings.

The same tension exists in digital connectivity. The Department of Information and Communications Technology’s (DICT) rollout of 19,000 free Wi-Fi sites and SIM distribution efforts reflect strong investment. The eGov app, with over 40 services, shows real progress in digital governance. Yet, last-mile access, power supply issues, and integration gaps still threaten long-term success.

On energy, the SONA highlighted solar initiatives and a plan for 200 new power plants by 2028. But the Department of Energy reports over 60% of approved renewable projects haven’t even broken ground. Fossil fuels still dominate, accounting for 58% of energy generation in 2024. “Green energy” remains more rhetoric than reality—slowed by bureaucracy and private interests.

In water services, Marcos’ call for accountability is overdue. Utilities like Maynilad and Manila Water have long underperformed. But without addressing the root issue—the privatization of water—no reform will resolve high tariffs or shortages. Real water justice demands bold steps: remunicipalization and citizen oversight.

Education was also a key concern. The President acknowledged poor PISA scores, rolled out the Academic Recovery and Accessible Learning (ARAL) program, and promised new classrooms. While these efforts are commendable, deeper problems remain: underpaid teachers, outdated curricula, and a broken K–12 system. Despite new laws, most Grade 3 students remain years behind, and 62% of teachers are still teaching outside their fields. The response feels more like triage than transformation. Although the president promised to add compensation for teachers' overtime and overload work, a promise alone does not equate to effective action.

Mental health programs and anti-bullying reforms were mentioned, but lack muscle. Though laws have passed and plantilla positions created, 94% of counselors say they cannot meet student needs. Many schools still lack even basic policies. Without proper staffing and enforcement, these are promises without protection.

While the recognition of teachers’ welfare is encouraging, the SONA’s silence on healthcare workers, especially nurses, is glaring. Inadequate pay, delayed benefits, and migration for better opportunities continue to worsen the nursing crisis. The pay gap between first-year medical residents in the Philippines (₱720,000) and the U.S. (₱3,000,000) underscores the urgency. The neglect of these professionals—crucial even beyond the pandemic—further strains a struggling healthcare system.

Healthcare wins like expanded PhilHealth dialysis and cancer subsidies do offer hope—but aren’t yet universally accessible. Transport and regional inequalities still bar access. Decentralization is needed, but painfully slow.

Even in agriculture, where bold action was promised, the gaps are wide. The goal of ₱20-per-kilo rice drew skepticism, especially from farmers like Mario Satulan in Nueva Ecija. Selling palay at such low rates would mean loss. The true cost of milling and logistics brings rice prices to ₱35–₱42 per kilo. Government data confirms that ₱20 rice is only possible under heavy subsidies, limited to KADIWA centers covering less than 1% of consumption. It’s a political spectacle posing as policy.

Even sincere interventions—like distributing equipment and seeds—lack structural reform. The food system remains import-dependent. Farmers’ wages are stagnant. The law continues to favor big importers. The President’s silence on land reform, corporate agri-consolidation, and land grabbing makes “agricultural recovery” sound hollow. Charity without sovereignty is pacification—not progress.

Even educational pride was distorted. The President claimed 114 universities were internationally recognized, yet the Times Higher Education Impact Rankings listed 121. While the discrepancy seems small, it highlights the importance of accurate representation. Celebrating progress is good—but not when it’s misleading. Accountability strengthens, not undermines, the prestige of institutions striving for global excellence.

Worse, the SONA ignored rising social dangers like online gambling, POGO operations, and their ties to human trafficking. The failure to address these crises casts doubt on the government’s narrative of peace and prosperity, revealing a disconnect from everyday realities.

“Sa mga matitinding hamon na binabato ng pamahalaan at hinaharang ng ating mundo ngayon, nasa likod ninyo ang pamahalaan.” These words, meant to reassure, in truth expose a disturbing truth: a government that stands behind its people is not leading—it is hiding. It watches from the shadows. It avoids the frontlines, where courage, responsibility, and decisive action are demanded.

And when a government hides behind its citizens too long, it should know that the people will turn around. And when they do, they will not just expect leadership. They will demand accountability—for every moment spent in the shadows.

We must resist the trend of political complacency. It is no longer enough for our leaders to simply claim they are doing their best. What matters are results, transparency, and the courage to face hard truths. Good intentions without measurable outcomes are not leadership—they are performance.

As engaged citizens, we must demand more—more honesty, more clarity, and more accountability. By holding those in power to the same standards we expect from doctors, teachers, engineers, and workers, we reaffirm the foundation of our democracy: that accountability exempts no one.
