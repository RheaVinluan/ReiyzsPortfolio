Suits, Microphones, and the Joke That Never Gets Old

Published on: The QCU Times Publication

Every March, the Philippines celebrates Women’s Month. Government agencies post purple banners, institutions publish messages about empowerment, and politicians deliver speeches praising the strength and contributions of Filipino women.

Yet every year, the same contradiction resurfaces: the very men who stand behind microphones to celebrate women are often the same ones who reduce them to jokes.

It would be absurd—if it were not so routine.

Filipino women continue to hear rape jokes, sexual remarks, and degrading comments from men in public office—statements that would be unacceptable in any professional environment. But in Philippine politics, these remarks are often met with laughter, applause, and even political support.

In 2016, while campaigning for the presidency, Rodrigo Duterte joked about the rape and murder of an Australian missionary during a Davao prison riot, saying that she was so beautiful that “the mayor should have been first.” The remark sparked international outrage, yet it did not stop his rise to the presidency.

Years later, misogynistic remarks continue to surface from men in power. Senator Robin Padilla drew criticism after commenting about marital relations, suggesting that when a husband “is in the mood,” a wife should respond to her husband’s sexual desire—remarks widely criticized for implying entitlement to a woman’s body even within marriage.

In Quezon City, Representative Bong Suntay also faced criticism after publicly sharing his “imagination” about actress Anne Curtis, turning a successful woman into the subject of a male fantasy rather than treating her with basic respect.

Lawyer Ferdinand Topacio similarly attacked actress Jasmine Curtis-Smith for posting bikini photos online, suggesting that such photos were meant for men to desire her—an outdated mindset that polices women’s confidence while excusing men’s entitlement.

These remarks are not isolated incidents. They reflect a culture where misogyny from powerful men is normalized, defended, and sometimes even celebrated.

What is perhaps more troubling than the remarks themselves is the reaction they receive.

People laugh.
Supporters defend them as “just jokes.”
And voters continue to reward these men with power.

Misogyny in politics does not survive simply because politicians say these things—it survives because people tolerate them.

When voters cheer, dismiss criticism, or continue electing leaders who joke about rape or sexualize women in public, they become part of the system that keeps these attitudes alive.

And if these are the things said openly in front of microphones and cameras, one must ask: what else is said when the cameras are off? If women can be reduced to objects, fantasies, or punchlines in public speeches, it is not difficult to imagine the kinds of remarks that may be made privately—moments where women are degraded without witnesses.

In any other profession, remarks like these could lead to investigation, suspension, or termination. But in politics, they are delivered while wearing barongs and tailored suits—and somehow the suit becomes a shield from accountability.

But a formal suit does not make a person respectable.

For many women, this environment sends a troubling message: that being beautiful, confident, and visible in public can feel like a curse. Instead of being respected, it invites objectification, ridicule, and entitlement from men who believe their power excuses their words.

Women’s Month should not simply be about greetings, hashtags, or ceremonial speeches.

Because if rape jokes can coexist with election victories, then the celebration itself begins to sound hollow.

Respect for women cannot exist only in slogans. It must exist in the standards we demand from those who seek to lead us.

Otherwise, the most laughable thing about Women’s Month is not the jokes politicians make about women.

It is the fact that we keep electing the people who tell them.
