The “Mental Health” Card

Published on: The QCU Times Publication

Along the long and merciless road of life, sharp rocks, venomous snakes, fallen logs, and endless wilderness wait at every turn to wound the weary traveler. Some scratches are shallow and fleeting, forgotten like dust carried away by the wind. But others cut deeper — carving themselves into flesh until they become bleeding wounds and permanent scars etched into the soul. Knees tremble, bodies collapse onto unforgiving ground, and hearts grow tired from carrying pain no one else can see. Yet even in the darkest moments, every person is faced with a choice: to rise again, to reach for a helping hand, or to suffer alone in silence.

But there are roads far more dangerous than the open battlefield. There are hidden alleys drowned in shadows, where pain slithers like blood beneath the skin, whispering invitations toward places unknown. These places feel like bottomless wells of despair — dark, cold, and endless — where hope becomes distant and the thought of returning to the light feels impossible. Some people become trapped there for so long that the darkness no longer frightens them; it becomes part of them.

There are nights when silence becomes louder than screams, when exhaustion no longer comes from the body but from carrying emotions too heavy for the heart to contain. Smiles become disguises carefully stitched together to survive another day, while behind them are storms no one notices. Many people are not asking to be saved — they are simply begging not to drown alone.

And sometimes, the most tragic wounds are not the ones we carry, but the ones we choose to inflict on others.

There are people who take their bleeding scars and sharpen them into weapons, pressing their pain into another person’s skin until the battlefield of life becomes poisoned with betrayal, manipulation, and destruction. Instead of healing, they drag others into the same abyss they once fell into. Instead of seeking peace, they use suffering as justification for causing more suffering. Their tears become masks, their cries for help become carefully crafted tools, and the scars they once begged others to understand become chains wrapped around innocent hearts.

To struggle is human, and those drowning in pain deserve compassion. But compassion should never require another person to bleed in silence.

Deep scars should never become excuses to wound another soul.

Every person is fighting a war invisible to the eyes. Every heart carries battles untold, grief hidden behind smiles, and scars concealed beneath silence. But pain should never be used to manipulate, to betray, or to destroy someone who only wished to understand. Mental health is meant to be a call for compassion, healing, and humanity — not a shield to hide cruelty behind, nor a weapon used to silence accountability.

Healing is not found in passing wounds from one soul to another. It is found in the courage to break the cycle — to choose kindness even when bitterness feels easier, to choose honesty instead of manipulation, and to choose responsibility despite the weight of one’s suffering. The world already carries enough hidden pain; it does not need more people turning their scars into blades.

Perhaps this piece is too figurative for some to fully understand, but its message remains painfully simple this Mental Health Month:

Do not use your suffering as a reason to ruin another person’s life.

We cannot continue advocating for mental health awareness while allowing the “mental health” card to be used as justification for betrayal, emotional harm, and the destruction of others. True healing does not come from pulling people into darkness with us. True healing begins when we choose, despite our wounds, to stop the cycle of pain instead of passing it on.
