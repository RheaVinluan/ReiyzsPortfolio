A Nation Remembering — or Just Repeating?

Published on: The QCU Times Publication

Rizal Day should be a national pause button, not just another long weekend. Each year we gather around monuments, listen to speeches, and repeat well-worn lines about heroism — yet how deeply do we reflect on what José Rizal actually asked of us? Are we willing to question our own habits, our politics, and our priorities the way he questioned the colonial order of his time? If this day is meant to shape our conscience, what does our response say about who we are becoming?

Rizal’s journey — from a thoughtful child in Calamba to a physician, writer, and reformist in Europe and Asia — reminds us that courage can begin with curiosity. He immersed himself in learning not for vanity, but to understand his people’s suffering. Through Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo, he confronted abuses and hypocrisy, challenging Filipinos to see themselves honestly. His critique was sharp, but always paired with hope: a belief that reason, education, and moral discipline could rebuild society. Today, do we still value learning as a tool for liberation, or merely as a ticket to status? How often do we choose silence because it is safe, rather than truth because it is necessary?

Despite advocating peaceful reform, Rizal was executed on December 30, 1896, in Bagumbayan — an attempt by the colonial regime to extinguish dissent. Instead, his death awakened a deeper sense of nationhood and helped ignite the struggle for independence, earning him recognition as our national hero. But if his life was a call to conscience, how do we measure ourselves now? Do we resist injustice when we see it in everyday spaces — in government queues, classrooms, workplaces, and online — or do we shrug and move on?

To honor Rizal meaningfully, remembrance must become responsibility. What would it look like if we confronted corruption without excuses, protected truth from disinformation, and demanded fairness from leaders — and from ourselves? Are we investing in education as a public duty, or allowing inequity to widen? Do our votes reflect long-term principles, or short-term favors? These questions are uncomfortable, but they are precisely the kind that Rizal’s writings insist we keep asking.

Rizal believed a nation is built by citizens who cultivate character, compassion, and courage. Commemoration alone cannot sustain that vision. The challenge of Rizal Day is to transform admiration into action and nostalgia into resolve. If we truly remember him, how will it change the choices we make tomorrow — in our families, communities, and institutions? And perhaps the most important question of all: when future generations look back at us, will they say we lived up to the freedom his sacrifice helped make possible?
