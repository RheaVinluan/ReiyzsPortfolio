Truth on Trial: Why Fact-Checking Matters More Than Ever

Published on: The QCU Times Publication

Every 2nd of April, on International Fact-Checking Day, the world is reminded of a responsibility that has become increasingly urgent in the digital age: verify before you believe. Today, information travels faster than ever, but truth often struggles to keep up. The use of artificial intelligence (AI) has rapidly taken over the digital world. These technologies are often used in luring some facts with carefully crafted stories and realistic prompts in AI technologies to make it look like reality. Through these, misinformation is widely crafted, posted, and believed by those who cannot distinguish facts from lies.

The scale of the problem is alarming. Studies show that a majority of Filipinos are concerned about fake news online, with surveys indicating that around 63% worry about distinguishing real news from false information. Social media platforms have also become major news sources, with many Filipinos relying on them for political updates and current events. When information spreads through platforms used by millions, falsehoods can travel faster than verified facts, shaping public opinion before corrections even appear. And the damage is cumulative. Fact-checkers in the Philippines have documented hundreds of false claims in a single year, with at least 12% involving AI-generated or manipulated images, videos, or audio—showing how technology is making deception even more sophisticated.

But misinformation does not spread on its own. It survives because it benefits certain actors. In fact, surveys show that many Filipinos themselves identify politicians as among the biggest sources of misleading information. This raises uncomfortable questions: Why do some public officials only condemn fake news when they are the target of it? Why is there silence when false narratives work in their favor? When misinformation boosts popularity, rewrites history, or shields leaders from accountability, the incentive to challenge it disappears.

The effects are not limited to online arguments. Misinformation divides communities, distorts elections, and erodes trust in institutions. When people can no longer agree on basic facts, democracy becomes fragile. Fact-checking, therefore, is not just the responsibility of journalists and researchers—it is a civic duty shared by everyone who consumes and shares information online.

International Fact-Checking Day is a reminder that truth requires effort. In a time when lies can be generated, amplified, and believed within seconds, the real question is no longer whether misinformation exists—it is whether society is willing to challenge it. If truth becomes optional in public discourse, what kind of future will democracy have? Fact-checking is more than verifying claims; it is an act of defending truth in an age where deception has become dangerously easy.
